What is SIDS?

Sudden Infant Death Syndrome (SIDS) is the sudden, unexplained death of an apparently healthy baby less than one year old. It is sometimes called "crib death" because it often occurs during sleep.

The Triple Risk Model

Current research suggests SIDS results from the convergence of three factors — known as the "Triple Risk Model." No single factor causes SIDS on its own.

Understanding this model helps explain why SIDS cannot be fully predicted or prevented with any single measure, but why risk reduction strategies are meaningful and important.

The three factors are: a vulnerable infant (subtle biological differences in brainstem development), a critical developmental period (typically 1–4 months), and an outside stressor such as stomach sleeping or soft bedding that might trigger failure of an arousal reflex.

⚠️

Important Note

SIDS cannot currently be predicted, diagnosed before death, or definitively prevented. These educational guidelines are designed to reduce risk — not eliminate it. A SIDS death is never a parent's fault.

Key Facts About SIDS

  • SIDS is the leading cause of death in infants 1–12 months
  • It typically occurs during sleep, often between midnight and 6 AM
  • More common in male infants (60% of cases)
  • Higher risk in premature or low birth weight babies
  • Most SIDS deaths occur between 1 and 4 months of age
  • African American and Native American infants face higher rates
  • SIDS is not caused by suffocation, choking, or vomiting
  • It is not contagious or hereditary
  • Vaccines do not cause SIDS
  • Apnea monitors are not proven to prevent SIDS

Risk Factors

Understanding risk factors can help parents make informed decisions. Many risk factors are modifiable — meaning you can take steps to reduce them.

👶

Age

Risk is highest between 1 and 4 months of age. About 90% of SIDS deaths occur in the first 6 months of life. The risk decreases significantly after 6 months.

😴

Sleep Position

Stomach sleeping increases risk significantly. Side sleeping is also riskier than back sleeping. Always place babies on their backs to sleep until age 1.

🚫

Smoke Exposure

Prenatal or postnatal exposure to cigarette smoke significantly increases SIDS risk. This includes secondhand smoke exposure in the home environment.

🌡️

Overheating

Babies who become too hot during sleep face elevated risk. Overdressing, heavy blankets, and warm room temperatures are contributing factors.

🏥

Prenatal Factors

Premature birth, low birth weight, multiple birth (twins, triplets), and inadequate prenatal care are associated with higher SIDS risk.

🛏️

Sleep Environment

Soft bedding, pillows, bumper pads, loose objects in the crib, and bed-sharing on soft surfaces all increase risk. A firm, flat surface is safest.

Safe Sleep Guidelines

The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) recommends these evidence-based safe sleep practices. Together, they represent the most effective risk-reduction strategy available.

1

Back to Sleep, Every Time

Always place your baby on their back to sleep — for naps and at night — until their first birthday. Even if baby can roll over, start them on their back.

2

Firm, Flat Sleep Surface

Use a firm, flat sleep surface such as a safety-approved crib, bassinet, or play yard with a tight-fitting sheet. Avoid inclined sleepers and positioners.

3

Room-Share, Don't Bed-Share

Share your room — ideally for at least 6 months — but on a separate sleep surface. Room-sharing reduces SIDS risk by up to 50% without the risks of bed-sharing.

4

Keep the Crib Bare

No pillows, blankets, bumper pads, soft toys, or positioners in the sleep space. A wearable blanket (sleep sack) is a safe alternative to loose blankets.

5

Avoid Smoke & Alcohol Exposure

Do not smoke during pregnancy or allow smoking near your baby. Avoid alcohol and sedating medications when you will be sharing a sleep space with your baby.

6

Breastfeed if Possible

Breastfeeding, even for a short time, can reduce SIDS risk by up to 50%. The protective effect is stronger with exclusive breastfeeding for at least 2 months.

7

Consider a Pacifier

Offering a pacifier at sleep time reduces SIDS risk. If breastfeeding, wait until 3–4 weeks. Don't force it — if baby doesn't want it, that's okay.

8

Keep Vaccinations Current

Evidence shows that vaccinations are protective against SIDS, reducing risk by about 50%. Vaccines do not cause SIDS — this is a thoroughly debunked myth.

9

Avoid Sleep Devices & Monitors Marketed to Prevent SIDS

No commercial device, including home monitors, has been proven to prevent SIDS. The AAP does not recommend these products as SIDS prevention tools.

Back to Sleep vs. Tummy Time

Back sleeping for sleep doesn't mean avoiding tummy time altogether. Supervised tummy time during wake hours is important for your baby's development.

🛌

Back Sleeping

For all sleep — naps and nighttime. Back sleeping allows better arousal from sleep and reduces risk of airway obstruction. This applies for the entire first year, even once baby can roll.

✓ Safest for Sleep
⏰

Tummy Time

Supervised, awake tummy time starting from birth (a few minutes, 2–3 times daily, building up to 30+ minutes total by 3 months). Essential for neck strength, motor skills, and head shape. Never leave baby unattended during tummy time.

Awake & Supervised Only

Room Temperature & Overheating

Overheating is a recognized SIDS risk factor. Keeping your baby comfortably warm — not hot — is an important part of safe sleep.

Ideal Sleep Temperature Range

<65°F / 18°C Too Cool
65–70°F / 18–21°C Ideal Range
71–74°F / 22–23°C Too Warm
>74°F / 23°C Danger Zone
Dress baby in one more layer than you'd wear to be comfortable
Check the back of baby's neck — it should feel warm but not sweaty
Use a sleep sack instead of loose blankets
Avoid hats during indoor sleep — babies release heat through their heads
No electric blankets, heating pads, or heated mattresses
Keep crib away from direct sunlight, radiators, and vents

When to Seek Medical Attention

Your instincts as a parent matter. When in doubt, call. These are general guidelines — if something feels wrong, don't wait.

Call 911 Immediately

Emergency Signs

  • Baby is not breathing or breathing is very irregular
  • Skin is blue, gray, or pale (cyanosis)
  • Baby is limp and unresponsive
  • Seizure activity or sustained abnormal movements
  • Baby is choking and cannot breathe
Call Your Doctor Today

Concerning but Not Immediate

  • Persistent low fever (especially in newborns under 3 months)
  • Persistent unusual breathing sounds
  • Unusual lethargy or difficulty waking
  • Consistently low SpO₂ readings on Bundl app
  • Significant changes in sleep pattern or feeding
Mention at Next Visit

Worth Discussing

  • Frequent nighttime waking beyond expected pattern
  • Difficulty settling for sleep consistently
  • Temperature trends you've noticed on Bundl
  • Heart rate patterns you'd like explained
  • General concerns about baby's sleep development
